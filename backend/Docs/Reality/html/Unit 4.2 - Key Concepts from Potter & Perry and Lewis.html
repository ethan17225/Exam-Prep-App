Key Concepts from Potter & Perry and Lewis (Unit 4.2 Content)

Potter & Perry Chapter 26 - The Experience of Loss, Death, and Grief

Understanding Loss and Grief

  • Loss: Any actual or perceived removal of a person, object, role, function, idea, or environment that has meaning for an individual, leading to a grief response shaped by values, relationships, and context. [cite: 100]
  • Types of Loss: Includes actual vs perceived loss, maturational loss (normal life transitions), situational loss (sudden events), and multiple losses (often seen with chronic illness/disability). [cite: 101]
  • Losses can involve external objects, environments, significant others, aspects of self, or life itself; the degree of grief depends on the value and attachment placed on the loss. [cite: 102, 103]
  • Grief: The total response (physical, emotional, cognitive, social, spiritual) to loss. [cite: 104]
  • Bereavement: The state of having lost a significant other and living through grief and mourning. [cite: 105]
  • Grief is individual and non-linear; people oscillate between periods of intensity, eventually adapting with new meanings and coping strategies. [cite: 106]

Theories and Types of Grief

  • Classic grief theories: Kübler-Ross's stages (denial, anger, bargaining, depression, acceptance), Bowlby and Parkes' attachment phases, the dual-process model, meaning-making, and continuing bonds. [cite: 108]
  • Normal (uncomplicated) grief: Involves manageable feelings (sadness, anger, guilt), thoughts (disbelief), behaviours (crying), and physical sensations. [cite: 109]
  • Anticipatory grief: Occurs when loss is expected (e.g., terminal illness); it may aid adjustment but can increase anxiety and risk of complicated grief. [cite: 110, 111]
  • Complicated or prolonged grief: Chronic, intense, and disabling grief reactions causing functional impairment that require specialized intervention. [cite: 112]
  • Disenfranchised grief: Arises when a loss is not socially recognized or supported (e.g., overdose death, pet loss), limiting expression. [cite: 114]

Death, "Good Death," and Factors

  • Biological death: Defined by lack of oxygen; categorized as neurological death or circulatory death. Social/psychological death capture the loss of roles and internal acceptance. [cite: 116, 117]
  • "Good death": Highly individualized, generally involving symptom control, closure, preserved dignity, and feeling seen as a person. [cite: 118, 119]
  • Dying Person's Bill of Rights: Emphasizes control, being treated humanely, expression of emotions, honest answers, and not dying alone. [cite: 120]
  • Influencing Factors: Development, prior experiences, coping skills, socioeconomic status, culture, and spirituality all influence responses to loss. [cite: 121, 122]
  • Children need honest communication; older adults may face multiple concurrent losses, risking prolonged grief. [cite: 123, 124]
  • Culturally safe care is heavily emphasized, particularly for Indigenous peoples given colonial histories. [cite: 125, 126]

Nursing Process for Loss, Death, and Grief

  • Assessment: Focuses on the meaning of the loss, grief type/stage, symptoms, coping strategies, and cultural/spiritual context. [cite: 128]
  • Nursing Approach: Avoid assumptions, encourage narratives ("Tell me what this has been like"), watch non-verbal cues. [cite: 129]
  • Interventions: Therapeutic communication, promoting hope, supporting coping, and facilitating hospice/palliative care. [cite: 130, 131]
  • End of Life & Post-Death Care: Support symptom management, explain signs of impending death, assist with advance care planning, respectful care of the body, and family support. [cite: 132, 133]
  • Nurses must engage in debriefing and self-care to manage their own vulnerability to grief. [cite: 135, 136]

Potter & Perry Chapter 29 - Spirituality in Health and Healthcare

Core Definitions & Context

  • Spirituality: A way of being involving connectedness to self, others, nature, or a higher power, providing meaning/purpose; may or may not be linked to religion. [cite: 138, 139]
  • Religion: An organized system of beliefs/practices centred on a deity/ultimate reality. [cite: 140, 141]
  • Faith: Affiliation with a specific religion or a personal relationship with a higher power. [cite: 142, 143]
  • Spirituality and religion are considered social determinants of health linked to coping and resilience, though discrimination (e.g., Islamophobia) also impacts health. [cite: 148, 149]

Nursing and Spiritual Care

  • Holistic nursing: Recognizes the unity of mind, body, spirit, and social context; spiritual care is integral, not an "add-on". [cite: 158, 159]
  • Professional ethics: Require treating all patients with dignity, avoiding discrimination, and refraining from proselytizing. [cite: 161]
  • Assessment Tools: Use frameworks like the 2Q-SAM ("What's most important to you right now?") or FICA (Faith, Importance, Community, Address). [cite: 164, 165, 166]
  • Layered care model: Includes primary spiritual care (all clinicians), supportive (extra training), and specialized (chaplains, Elders) for deep spiritual distress. [cite: 171]
  • Self-care: Reflective practice about personal biases, mindfulness, and organizational supports are essential for nurses. [cite: 174, 175, 176]

Lewis Chapter 2 - Cultural Competence and Health Equity

  • Culture: Shapes health beliefs, illness expressions, and decision-making; often conflicting with dominant Western biomedical models. [cite: 179, 180]
  • Cultural competence & safety: Involves recognizing power imbalances, engaging in cultural humility, and partnering with communities. [cite: 181]
  • Nurses must integrate assessing multiple healing systems (Indigenous medicine, Ayurveda, homeopathy, etc.). [cite: 183, 184]
  • For Indigenous peoples, the loss of land, language, and culture has produced profound health inequities, necessitating trust-building and respecting Indigenous knowledge. [cite: 187, 188]

Lewis Chapter 11 - Substance Use

  • Substance use continuum: Ranges from non-use to low-risk use to substance use disorder (SUD); routine assessment is crucial. [cite: 192, 193]
  • SUD involves cognitive, behavioural, and psychological symptoms indicating impaired control and tolerance/withdrawal. [cite: 194, 195]
  • Common substances: Alcohol, tobacco, opioids, cannabis, stimulants, sedatives. [cite: 196, 197]
  • The opioid overdose crisis has intensified, making hospital settings vital points of contact for harm reduction. [cite: 201, 202]
  • Nursing priorities: Trauma- and harm-reduction-oriented care, brief interventions, motivational interviewing, and symptom management. [cite: 203]

Lewis Chapter 13 - Palliative and End-of-Life Care

  • Palliative care: Approach to improve quality of life by treating pain and other physical/psychosocial problems early in the disease trajectory. [cite: 207, 208]
  • End-of-life care: Focuses on comfort and preparation when death is expected soon. [cite: 208]
  • Goals of care: Symptom control, autonomy, advance care planning, and addressing spiritual/emotional needs. [cite: 209]
  • Physical manifestations of impending death: Decreased intake, weakness, altered breathing (Cheyne-Stokes), and altered circulation. [cite: 210]
  • Post-death care involves respectful care of the body and supporting family grief rituals. [cite: 213]

Lewis Chapter 24 - Visual and Auditory Conditions

Visual Conditions

  • Cataracts: Opacity of the lens causing decreased vision and glare; managed with visual aids or surgical lens extraction. [cite: 218, 219, 220]
  • Macular Degeneration (AMD): Causes central vision loss. Dry AMD is gradual; wet AMD involves neovascularization. Management includes vitamins, anti-VEGF, and smoking cessation. [cite: 223, 224, 226, 227]
  • Glaucoma: Optic nerve damage linked to increased intraocular pressure. Open-angle (gradual peripheral loss) vs angle-closure (sudden pain, halos). Treatment includes lifelong topical medications or surgery. [cite: 229, 230, 231, 232]

Auditory & Balance Conditions

  • Otitis Media: Inflammation of the middle ear; can be acute (fever, pain) or chronic. Treated with analgesia, antibiotics, or surgery. [cite: 235, 236, 237]
  • Ménière's Disease: Episodic vertigo, hearing loss, tinnitus. Nursing focuses on fall prevention and sodium restriction. [cite: 240, 241, 242]
  • BPPV: Brief vertigo triggered by head position; treated with repositioning maneuvers. [cite: 243, 244]
  • Hearing Loss/Deafness: Requires individualized communication strategies, environmental modifications, and deep respect for Deaf culture. [cite: 246, 248, 249]
