Nursing Exam Study Guide

Fluids, Electrolytes, Acid-Base & Practice Concepts

1. FLUID COMPARTMENTS & MOVEMENT

  • Total Body Water (TBW): Makes up ~60% of adult body weight.
  • Intracellular Fluid (ICF): Fluid inside cells, containing 2/3 of TBW.
  • Extracellular Fluid (ECF): Fluid outside cells (1/3 of TBW). Includes interstitial fluid (between cells) and intravascular fluid (blood plasma).
Starling Forces (Capillary Fluid Exchange):
  • Hydrostatic Pressure: Pushes water out (Capillary hydrostatic pressure pushes water into tissues).
  • Oncotic Pressure: Pulls water in (Plasma oncotic pressure attracts water back into vessels).
Edema: Excessive accumulation of fluid in interstitial spaces.
Causes to remember: 1) Increased capillary hydrostatic pressure (e.g., heart failure, venous obstruction), 2) Decreased plasma oncotic pressure (e.g., liver disease, malnutrition, loss of albumin), 3) Increased capillary permeability (e.g., burns, inflammation), and 4) Lymphatic obstruction.

2. HORMONAL REGULATION

  • Aldosterone: Secreted when blood volume/pressure drops or sodium decreases. Promotes sodium and water retention and potassium excretion in the kidneys.
  • Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH): Released when plasma osmolality increases (blood is too concentrated) or blood volume drops. It promotes water retention in the kidneys.
  • Natriuretic Peptides (ANH/BNP): Antagonists to the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system. They cause vasodilation and promote sodium and water excretion, lowering blood pressure.

3. SODIUM & TONICITY IMBALANCES (Normal Na⁺: 135-145 mmol/L)

  • Isotonic Alteration: Proportional loss/gain of water and electrolytes. Example: Hemorrhage causes isotonic hypovolemia; excess IV saline causes isotonic hypervolemia.
  • Hypernatremia (>145 mmol/L): A hypertonic state where ECF is too concentrated, drawing water out of cells (cells shrink).
    Symptoms: Thirst, bounding pulse, confusion, muscle twitching, and hyper-reflexia.
  • Hyponatremia (<135 mmol/L): A hypotonic state where ECF is too dilute, forcing water into cells (cells swell).
    Symptoms: Lethargy, confusion, seizures, and severe risk of cerebral edema.

4. POTASSIUM IMBALANCES (Normal K⁺: 3.5-5.1 mmol/L)

  • Hypokalemia (<3.5 mmol/L): Causes cell hyperpolarization.
    Symptoms: Skeletal muscle weakness, cardiac dysrhythmias, and decreased smooth muscle tone (paralytic ileus).
    Note: IV potassium must be given slowly (max 20 mmol/hr) and NEVER by IV push.
  • Hyperkalemia (>5.0 mmol/L): Causes cell hypopolarization.
    Symptoms: Muscle weakness/paralysis and severe bradydysrhythmias/cardiac arrest. Often occurs alongside acidosis or renal failure.

5. ACID-BASE IMBALANCES (Normal pH: 7.35-7.45)

Normal ABGs: PaCO₂: 35-45 mmHg, HCO₃⁻: 22-26 mmol/L.

  • Metabolic Acidosis (pH <7.35, HCO₃⁻ <22): Caused by ketoacidosis, lactic acidosis, or renal failure.
    Compensation: Kussmaul respirations (deep, rapid breathing to blow off CO₂).
  • Metabolic Alkalosis (pH >7.45, HCO₃⁻ >26): Caused by vomiting or diuretic use.
    Symptoms: Muscle cramps, slow ventilation (to retain CO₂).
  • Respiratory Acidosis (pH <7.35, PaCO₂ >45): Caused by hypoventilation (head injury, pneumonia, COPD).
    Compensation: Kidneys retain bicarbonate.
  • Respiratory Alkalosis (pH >7.45, PaCO₂ <35): Caused by hyperventilation (anxiety, hypoxemia).
    Symptoms: Dizziness, tingling (paresthesias).

6. CRITICAL LAB VALUES TO MEMORIZE

BUN: 2.9-8.3 mmol/L
Creatinine: 44-106 mcmol/L (varies slightly by sex)
Fasting Glucose: 4-6 mmol/L
HgbA1C: 4%-6%
WBC: 5,000–10,000/mm³
Platelets: 150,000-400,000/mm³
Coagulation: PTT: 60-70 sec | aPTT: 25-40 sec | INR: 0.8-1.2 (Therapeutic is 2-3)

7. REx-PN & PROFESSIONAL PRACTICE CONCEPTS

  • CAT Exam Format: Computer Adaptive Testing means questions change in difficulty based on your previous answers to estimate your passing standard.
  • Study Application (Bloom's Taxonomy): Focus not just on remembering, but on analyzing and applying knowledge to solve problems.
  • Legal/CNO Guidelines: Remember that a client's right to safe, competent, and ethical care is the highest priority. Make sure you understand the Code of Conduct, informed consent, and professional boundaries.
