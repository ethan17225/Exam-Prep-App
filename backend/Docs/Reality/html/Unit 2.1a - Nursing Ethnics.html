Nursing Ethics, Values & Patient Safety

Comprehensive Study Sheet

1. Key Concepts & Definitions

  • Values: Strong personal beliefs and ideals that a person or group believes to have merit.
  • Values Clarification: The process of personal reflection to appraise and understand personal values, helping to decide which values guide practice and resolve conflicts.
  • Ethics: The study of philosophical ideals of good conduct, character, motives, and right/wrong behavior.
  • Moral Distress: Occurs when a nurse cannot act according to their moral judgement due to systemic/situational constraints, risking personal integrity.
  • Fitness to Practice: Having physical, mental, and emotional capacity, plus skills/knowledge, to work safely and effectively.
  • Social Justice: Fairness focusing on equitable distribution of benefits/burdens in society to address health determinants and eliminate inequities.

2. Ethical Theories & Bioethical Principles

  • Deontology: Defines actions as right or wrong based on moral duties, principles, and rules, regardless of consequences.
  • Utilitarianism (Consequentialism): Values an action based on usefulness/outcomes, aiming for "greatest good for the greatest number".
  • Relational Ethics: Focuses on relationships (environment, embodiment, mutual respect, engagement).
Bioethics (Core Principles):
  • Autonomy: Respect for freedom, self-determination, and choice (e.g., informed consent).
  • Beneficence: Obligation to promote good and act in the patient's best interest.
  • Nonmaleficence: Commitment to do no harm and minimize risks.
  • Justice: Fairness and equity in treatment and resource distribution.

3. Professional Nursing Codes & Responsibilities

  • ICN Code of Ethics (4 Elements): Nurses and patients/people requiring care, Nurses and practice, Nurses and the profession, Nurses and global health.
  • CNA Code of Ethics (7 Primary Values): 1) Safe/compassionate care, 2) Promote health/well-being, 3) Promote informed decision-making, 4) Honour dignity, 5) Maintain privacy/confidentiality, 6) Promote justice, 7) Be accountable.
Core Nursing Commitments:
  • Responsibility: Reliability, dependability, performing duties correctly.
  • Accountability: Being answerable for actions, maintaining standards, competence, and fitness.
  • Advocacy: Acting for another, speaking for those who cannot, protecting rights.

4. Ethical Decision-Making & Key Practice Issues

  • Advance Care Planning (ACP): Patients communicate values/preferences for future treatments if unable to decide later.
  • Medical Assistance in Dying (MAiD): Legal framework for medically assisted death. Nurses provide MAiD under physician/NP direction (may conscientiously object based on provincial rules).
  • Futile Care: Interventions offering no foreseeable benefit or return to productive life; requires negotiating facts vs. patient values.
7 Steps to Analyze an Ethical Dilemma:
  1. Determine if it is an ethical dilemma.
  2. Gather all relevant info.
  3. Examine your own values.
  4. Verbalize the problem.
  5. Consider possible actions.
  6. Reflect on the outcome.
  7. Evaluate the action and outcome.

5. Patient Safety & Medication Administration

  • Patient Safety: Absence of preventable harm and reduction of healthcare risks to an acceptable minimum. Half of avoidable harm relates to medications.
  • Rights of Medication Administration: Right medication, dose, patient, route, time, frequency, documentation, reason, patient education, assessment, and right to refuse.
Handling Medication Errors:
  1. Prioritize patient treatment and safety.
  2. Report the error immediately.
  3. Complete an incident report.
Medication Reconciliation (Done at every care transition):
  1. Develop list of current home medications.
  2. Develop list of prescribed medications.
  3. Compare both lists.
  4. Make clinical decisions.
  5. Communicate the new list to caregivers and the patient.
