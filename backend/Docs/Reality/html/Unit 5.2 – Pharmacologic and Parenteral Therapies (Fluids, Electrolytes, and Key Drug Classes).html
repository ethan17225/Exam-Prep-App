Unit 5.2 - Pharmacologic and Parenteral Therapies (Fluids, Electrolytes, and Key Drug Classes)

Lilley Chapter 30 - Fluid and Electrolytes

Core Fluid Concepts and Compartments

  • Total body water (TBW): About 60% of adult body weight, distributed between intracellular fluid (ICF) and extracellular fluid (ECF).
  • ECF: Subdivided into intravascular fluid (plasma volume) and interstitial fluid (ISF). Transcellular fluid (e.g., CSF) is a specialized part of ISF.
  • Euvolemia: Normal fluid balance maintained via thirst, antidiuretic hormone (ADH), aldosterone, and renal function.

Osmolality, Tonicity, and Pressures

  • Osmolality: Concentration of particles in body fluids. Tonicity describes the effective osmolality of IV solutions relative to body fluids.
  • Isotonic solutions have similar osmolality to body fluids; hypotonic solutions have lower osmolality (fluid moves into cells), and hypertonic solutions have higher osmolality (fluid moves out of cells).
  • Colloid oncotic pressure (COP): The pulling pressure exerted by plasma proteins (primarily albumin) that draws fluid into the intravascular space.

Dehydration and Types of Fluid Loss

  • Dehydration: Excessive water loss, often with electrolyte imbalance.
  • Hypertonic dehydration: Water loss > sodium loss (e.g., fever, sweating) → cells shrink.
  • Hypotonic dehydration: Sodium loss > water loss (e.g., kidney insufficiency) → fluid shifts into cells.
  • Isotonic dehydration: Equal sodium and water loss (e.g., vomiting, diarrhea) → ECF volume deficit.

Crystalloids and Colloids

  • Crystalloids: IV fluids containing water and small solutes that expand ECF volume.
    • 0.9% NaCl (NS): Isotonic, primary resuscitation.
    • Lactated Ringer's: Isotonic balanced electrolyte solution.
    • 3% NaCl: Hypertonic (for severe hyponatremia; high-risk).
    • 0.45% NaCl: Hypotonic (shifts fluid into cells).
    • D5W: Isotonic in bag, hypotonic once dextrose is metabolized.
  • Colloids: Large-molecule solutions (e.g., albumin, dextran) that stay in vasculature, increase COP, and pull fluid from ISF. Used as plasma expanders. Risk: Dilutional coagulopathy and bleeding.

Blood Products

  • Used when >25% of blood volume is lost or to restore oxygen-carrying/clotting capacity.
  • PRBCs: For symptomatic anemia/Hgb deficits.
  • Whole blood: For major hemorrhage (>25% loss).
  • FFP & Cryoprecipitate: For clotting factor deficiencies.
  • Nursing: Verify type/crossmatch, 2-RN check, use ONLY 0.9% NaCl, and monitor VS closely for transfusion reactions.

Electrolyte Physiology and Replacement

  • Potassium (K): Major intracellular cation (3.5-5 mmol/L).
    • Hyperkalemia: Treated with IV bicarbonate, insulin + dextrose, calcium salts, or Kayexalate.
    • IV Potassium: High-alert! MUST be diluted. Never IV push. Max 10 mmol/hr peripheral.
  • Sodium (Na): Main extracellular cation (135-145 mmol/L). Regulates water distribution.
    • Severe hyponatremia may require 3% NaCl, but it is high-risk for osmotic demyelination if corrected too fast.

Key Drug Classes

Anticoagulants and Thrombolytics

  • Anticoagulants: (Heparin, warfarin, DOACs) Prevent clot formation/extension; do NOT dissolve existing clots.
  • Thrombolytics: (Alteplase) Actively lyse clots. Used in acute MI, ischemic stroke, massive PE.
  • Monitoring: aPTT for Heparin, PT/INR for Warfarin. Major risk is bleeding.

Cardiac Medications and Diuretics

  • Diuretics: (Loop, thiazide, potassium-sparing) Reduce volume/cardiac workload. Loop and thiazide risk hypokalemia; potassium-sparing risk hyperkalemia.
  • Nursing: Monitor daily weights, I&O, BP, and electrolytes (Na, K).

Antidiabetics and Insulins

  • Insulin: High-alert medication. Has specific onset, peak, and duration that must be coordinated with meals.
  • Nursing: Monitor for hypoglycemia (shakiness, confusion, sweating, tachycardia), rotate sites, and independent double-checks.

Bronchodilators

  • Classes include Beta2-agonists (rescue/maintenance), Anticholinergics (key in COPD), and Methylxanthines.
  • Nursing: Assess respiratory status, teach proper inhaler sequence (bronchodilator BEFORE steroid).

Opioids, Narcotics, Sedatives

  • Major Risks: Respiratory depression, excessive sedation, hypotension, and constipation.
  • Nursing: Assess pain and RR. Use lowest effective dose. Keep naloxone or flumazenil available. Provide bowel regimens.

Medications for Specific Circumstances

  • Epidural/Intrathecal: Regional analgesia. Risks include hypotension, motor block, and respiratory depression. Ensure strict aseptic technique and clear labeling.
  • Contraindicated in Pregnancy: Teratogenic meds (ACEIs/ARBs, warfarin, methotrexate, isotretinoin) must be avoided. Hormonal contraception like Depo-Provera is strictly for preventing pregnancy.
  • Injectable Depot Medications: Long-acting IM/SC injections (e.g., antipsychotics, contraceptives). Improves adherence but side effects cannot be quickly reversed.
