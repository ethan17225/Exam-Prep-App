Unit 5.1 - Basic Care and Comfort: Key Concepts from Lewis

Chapter 10 - Pain

Core Definitions & Impact

  • Pain definition: An unpleasant sensory and emotional experience associated with actual or potential tissue damage; it is subjective and best assessed by the patient's self-report[cite: 270, 271].
  • Multidimensional nature: Pain involves physiological, sensory-discriminative, motivational-affective, behavioural, cognitive-evaluative, and sociocultural dimensions[cite: 272].
  • Impact: Unrelieved pain is common and causes impaired recovery, disability, and reduced quality of life, with Indigenous peoples in Canada carrying a disproportionate burden of chronic pain[cite: 273, 274, 275].

Physiology and Types of Pain

  • Nociception: Involves four steps: transduction (conversion of noxious stimulus), transmission (conduction to the spinal cord/brain), perception (conscious awareness), and modulation (inhibition or facilitation)[cite: 277, 278].
  • Sensitization: Peripheral and central sensitization (e.g., wind-up) amplify nociceptive input; preventing this helps avoid chronic pain[cite: 278, 279].
  • Nociceptive pain: Arises from somatic or visceral tissue damage (e.g., arthritis, surgical incision) and usually responds well to opioids and non-opioids[cite: 280, 281].
  • Neuropathic pain: Results from nerve damage or abnormal processing (e.g., diabetic neuropathy), is often described as burning or shooting, and typically requires adjuvant medications[cite: 282, 283].
  • Acute vs. Chronic pain: Acute pain has a sudden onset related to injury and diminishes with healing. Chronic (persistent) pain lasts beyond expected healing (>3 months) and is linked to functional impairment, anxiety, and depression[cite: 284, 285].

Pain Assessment

  • Principles: Self-report is the most reliable indicator; physiologic/behavioural signs are not specific and must not replace self-report[cite: 287, 288].
  • Routine monitoring: Pain must be assessed routinely, at least once daily, and more frequently after acute changes or interventions[cite: 289].
  • Assessment tools: Utilize OPQRSTUV, PAIN frameworks, numeric/visual faces scales, and body diagrams[cite: 290, 291]. Alternative terms like "discomfort" or "soreness" may be needed for older adults[cite: 292].
  • Cognitive impairment: For these patients, behavioural indicators (facial expression, vocalization, consolability) and caregiver input are essential[cite: 294].

Pain Management

  • Multimodal approach: Combines pharmacologic and non-pharmacologic strategies with an interprofessional team and patient/family goal setting[cite: 298].
  • WHO analgesic ladder: Step 1 involves non-opioids for mild pain; Step 2 uses weak opioids + non-opioids for mild-moderate pain; Step 3 utilizes strong opioids for moderate-severe pain[cite: 299, 300].
  • Non-opioids & NSAIDs: Non-opioids have a ceiling effect and no physical dependence risks[cite: 301]. Acetaminophen requires strict adherence to daily limits to avoid hepatotoxicity[cite: 302]. NSAIDs block prostaglandin synthesis but carry GI, renal, and cardiovascular risks[cite: 303, 304].
  • Step 3 opioids: Have no ceiling effect but cause dose-limiting adverse effects (respiratory depression, nausea, sedation). Constipation must be proactively prevented[cite: 305, 306].
  • Adjuvant analgesics: Medications like antidepressants and anticonvulsants target neuropathic pain and muscle spasms[cite: 309, 310].

Ethical, Cultural, and Special Populations

  • Older adults: Emphasize "start low and go slow," limit NSAIDs, monitor for polypharmacy/falls, and utilize non-pharmacological strategies[cite: 314, 315].
  • Substance use disorders: These patients still have a right to adequate pain control; higher doses may be required due to tolerance, and mixed agonist-antagonists must be avoided to prevent withdrawal[cite: 316, 317].
  • Indigenous peoples: Often face discrimination (e.g., assumptions of drug-seeking) leading to undertreated pain. Culturally safe, trauma-informed care is vital[cite: 318, 319].

Chapter 19 - Fluid, Electrolyte, and Acid-Base Imbalance

  • Fluid distribution: Regulated by thirst, kidney function, and hormones (ADH, aldosterone, natriuretic peptides) across intracellular and extracellular compartments[cite: 322, 323].
  • IV Solutions: Isotonic, hypotonic, and hypertonic solutions differ in osmolality. Nurses must choose based on patient status (e.g., isotonic for volume depletion, hypertonic for severe hyponatremia)[cite: 324, 325].
  • Electrolyte imbalances: Common imbalances (Na, K, Ca, Mg, phosphate) have characteristic causes and manifestations (arrhythmias, mental-status changes) that guide interventions[cite: 326].
  • Acid-base balance: Reflected by ABGs. Respiratory imbalances relate to CO2 changes, while metabolic imbalances relate to HCO3-[cite: 327, 328].
  • Nursing care: Includes monitoring I&O, daily weights, vital signs, lab values, and providing patient teaching on diet and fluid restrictions[cite: 329].

Chapter 20 - Pre-operative Care

  • Goals: Establish a baseline, identify risks, and prepare patients physically and psychologically for surgery[cite: 333].
  • Assessment: Includes a comprehensive health history, physical exam, diagnostic review, and evaluating cardiovascular, respiratory, renal, hepatic, endocrine, and nutritional status[cite: 334, 335, 336, 337].
  • Preparation: Verifying informed consent, conducting pre-op teaching (breathing exercises, pain control expectations), and executing prep tasks like NPO status, skin antisepsis, and administering pre-op medications[cite: 338, 339].
  • Safety: Completing pre-op checklists and the "time-out" to confirm patient ID, allergies, and surgical site prior to OR transfer[cite: 340, 341].

Chapter 22 - Post-operative Care

  • Initial PACU priorities: Focus on Airway, Breathing, and Circulation (ABCs), neurologic status, and pain control[cite: 344]. Continuous monitoring for hypoventilation, hypoxemia, or airway obstruction is critical[cite: 345, 346].
  • Hemodynamic monitoring: Vital signs, skin temperature, urine output, and surgical drains are monitored to detect shock, hypovolemia, and bleeding[cite: 347, 348].
  • Complications: Early risks include atelectasis, DVT/PE, hemorrhage, urinary retention, and ileus. Later risks include infection and wound dehiscence[cite: 349, 350].
  • Interventions: Emphasize early mobilization, deep breathing/coughing, thrombosis prophylaxis, and adequate pain management[cite: 351].
  • Discharge teaching: Focuses on medication management, activity restrictions, wound care, and identifying symptoms that require urgent medical attention[cite: 352, 353].
