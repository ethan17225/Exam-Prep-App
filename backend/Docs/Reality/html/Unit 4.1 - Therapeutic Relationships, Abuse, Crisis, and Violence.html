Therapeutic Relationships, Abuse, Crisis, and Violence: One-Page Study Sheet [cite: 1]

1. Therapeutic Nurse-Client Relationship & Boundaries [cite: 2]

  • Therapeutic relationship: Goal-oriented, time-limited, and professional; focused on the client's health and recovery, not meeting the nurse's personal needs. [cite: 3]
  • Phases: Orientation (build trust, clarify roles and goals), Working (explore issues, promote change), Termination (review progress, plan for independence). [cite: 4]
  • Core nurse qualities: Empathy, respect, genuineness, and unconditional positive regard, expressed through active listening, attending behaviours, and cultural sensitivity. [cite: 5]
  • Boundaries: Invisible limits that keep the relationship professional and prevent role confusion, favoritism, and exploitation. [cite: 6]
  • Boundary warning signs: Secrecy, gifts, special favours, social media contact, [cite: 7] outside-of-care meetings, and over-involvement or under-involvement with a client. [cite: 8]
  • Nurse responsibilities: Establish and maintain boundaries, use a clear plan of care, explain the purpose and time limits of the relationship, maintain confidentiality, seek guidance, and end relationships appropriately. [cite: 8]

2. Protecting Clients from Abuse [cite: 10]

  • Abuse definition: Any behaviour in the nurse-client relationship that is violent, threatening, neglectful, humiliating, sexually inappropriate, or otherwise exploits the power imbalance. [cite: 11]
  • Forms of abuse: Physical, sexual, emotional/psychological, verbal, financial abuse, and neglect. [cite: 12]
  • Absolute prohibitions: Nurses must never engage in romantic or sexual relationships with current clients, and must not form sexual relationships with former clients for a set [cite: 13] period after the relationship ends (e.g., at least one year). [cite: 14]
  • Nurse duties: Recognize early signs of abuse, take immediate steps to stop or prevent harm, ensure safety, document clearly, and report to employers, CNO, and other regulators as required. [cite: 15]
  • Therapeutic stance: Believe the client, validate their experiences, avoid blaming, and work collaboratively on safety and support. [cite: 17]

3. Crisis and Disaster [cite: 18]

  • Crisis: A temporary state of psychological disequilibrium where usual coping strategies fail to resolve a stressor, leading to significant distress and impaired functioning. [cite: 19]
  • Types of crises: Maturational (developmental transitions), Situational (unexpected events such as illness, job loss), Adventitious or social (disasters, community violence, terrorism). [cite: 20]
  • Determinants of outcome: Person's perception of the event, social supports, and available coping skills/resources. [cite: 21]
  • Goals of crisis intervention: Ensure physical safety, reduce anxiety, restore the person to pre-crisis level of functioning or better, and prevent long-term psychological harm. [cite: 22]
  • Key nursing actions: Rapid assessment of risk (harm to self/others), simple and direct communication, problem-solving support, mobilizing social and community resources, and short-term, focused follow-up. [cite: 23]
  • Disaster context: Nurses contribute to triage, psychological first aid, basic needs, and ongoing monitoring of trauma reactions in individuals and communities. [cite: 24]

4. Suicide and Non-Suicidal Self-Injury [cite: 26]

  • Suicide risk factors: History of attempts, mental disorders (especially mood and substance-use disorders), hopelessness, recent loss or crisis, chronic pain, isolation, and access to lethal means. [cite: 27]
  • Warning signs: Talking about wanting to die, seeking means, expressing hopelessness or feeling like a burden, sudden calmness after severe depression, or significant behaviour change. [cite: 29]
  • Assessment tools: Use structured approaches (e.g., IS PATH WARM-style questions) plus clinical judgment to explore intent, plan, means, and protective factors. [cite: 30]
  • Prevention levels: Primary (population-level education and stigma reduction), Secondary (immediate intervention in crisis), Tertiary/postvention (support after attempts or death to reduce further risk). [cite: 32]
  • Non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI): Deliberate self-harm without intent to die, often used to regulate overwhelming emotions; [cite: 33] requires validation, assessment of function, and skills-based interventions, not punishment. [cite: 34]
  • Nursing priorities: Ensure immediate safety, remove or reduce access to means, maintain close observation when indicated, develop collaborative safety plans, and use non-judgmental therapeutic communication. [cite: 35]

5. Violence Across the Lifespan [cite: 36]

Child Abuse and Neglect [cite: 37]

  • Legal duty: Everyone in Ontario must immediately report any reasonable suspicion that a child is in need of protection to a children's aid society; [cite: 38] proof is not required. [cite: 39]
  • Forms: Physical, sexual, emotional abuse, neglect, and exposure to intimate partner violence. [cite: 40]
  • Reasonable grounds: Information that an average person, using normal judgment, would consider sufficient to suspect a child may be at risk; [cite: 41] new concerns must be reported each time. [cite: 42]
  • Nursing role: Recognize indicators (injuries, developmental delays, fearfulness, inconsistent stories), document objectively, report promptly, and collaborate with protection agencies. [cite: 43]

Elder Abuse [cite: 45]

  • Definition: Any act or failure to act within a relationship of trust that causes harm or risk of harm to an older adult (at home, in the community, or in care settings). [cite: 46]
  • Types: Physical, emotional/psychological, sexual, financial abuse, and neglect (e.g., withholding care, food, or medications). [cite: 48]
  • Warning signs: Unexplained injuries, poor hygiene, sudden changes in finances or living arrangements, fearfulness, withdrawal, or a caregiver speaking for or controlling the senior. [cite: 49]
  • Response: Listen, believe, avoid blaming, assess immediate safety, encourage safety planning, and connect to resources (Seniors Safety Line, community agencies, police). [cite: 50] Mandatory reporting rules apply in some care settings. [cite: 51]

Intimate Partner and Family Violence (IPV/FIPV) [cite: 52]

  • Definition: Patterns of physical, sexual, emotional, or financial abuse, threats, and coercive control within an intimate or family relationship. [cite: 53]
  • Examples: Insults and humiliation, isolation from supports, controlling money and decisions, threats to harm partner or children, destruction of property, using weapons or pets to intimidate. [cite: 55]
  • Health impacts: Physical injuries, chronic pain, sexually transmitted infections, mental health problems (anxiety, depression, PTSD), substance use, and increased risk of homicide. [cite: 57]
  • Peel context: Intimate partner violence has been identified as a serious and escalating issue, with specialized police units and the Safe Centre of Peel providing coordinated response and supports. [cite: 59]
  • Nursing responsibilities: Routinely and sensitively screen when safe, ensure privacy, validate the person's experience, assess lethality risk, help develop safety plans, document accurately, know local resources, and follow legal/reporting requirements. [cite: 61]

6. Quick Nursing Practice Checklist [cite: 63]

  • Maintain professionalism: Keep relationships time-limited, goal-oriented, and client-centred; monitor for boundary crossings and seek consultation early. [cite: 64]
  • Prioritize safety: In all situations of crisis, suicidality, abuse, or violence, assess immediate risk and act to protect the client and others. [cite: 65]
  • Know legal duties: Understand and follow mandatory reporting laws for child abuse, applicable rules for elder abuse and institutional settings, and professional obligations to report unsafe colleagues. [cite: 67]
  • Communicate therapeutically: Listen actively, validate feelings, avoid victim-blaming, and use clear, simple, and compassionate language. [cite: 69]
  • Collaborate and refer: Work with interprofessional teams (CAS, police, crisis lines, shelters, community agencies) and provide clients with information about resources and supports. [cite: 70]
  • Reflect on self: Be aware of personal reactions, biases, and vicarious trauma; [cite: 72] use supervision, debriefing, and self-care to support safe, ethical practice. [cite: 73]
