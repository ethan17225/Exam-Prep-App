Health Foundations & Assessment

Nursing Core Concepts Study Guide

1. Conceptualizations & Historical Approaches

  • Definitions of Health: Historically "absence of disease". WHO defines it positively as a state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being (a resource for everyday life).
  • Medical Approach: "Downstream thinking," focusing on physiological risk factors, pathology, and medical interventions.
  • Behavioural Approach (1974): Shifted focus to lifestyle/environment, placing responsibility on individual behaviours.
  • Socioenvironmental Approach (1986): "Upstream thinking," recognizing health is tied to social structures and environmental living conditions (poverty, hazards).

2. Health Promotion vs. Disease Prevention

  • Health Promotion: Enabling people to increase control over their health. Ottawa Charter: 1) Build healthy public policy, 2) Create supportive environments, 3) Strengthen community action, 4) Develop personal skills, 5) Reorient health services.
Levels of Disease Prevention:
Primary: Protects against disease before signs occur (e.g., immunizations).
Secondary: Early detection & prompt treatment to halt disease (e.g., screening).
Tertiary: Minimizes residual disability in convalescence (e.g., rehab).

3. Social Determinants of Health (SDOH)

  • Definition: Economic/social conditions shaping health. Income & social status influences most other determinants.
  • Key SDOH: Education, Employment, Early childhood, Food insecurity, Housing, Social exclusion, Indigenous status, Gender, Race, Disability.
  • Equity vs. Equality: Equity is the absence of unfair systems, aiming to increase access for all. Inequities are differences linked to systemic disadvantage.

4. The Health Interview & Communication

  • Purpose: Gather subjective data, build trust, understand goals.
  • Questions: Use open-ended for narrative info/rapport; use closed/direct for specific facts.
  • Inclusive Care: Recognize implicit biases. Use gender-inclusive language and self-identified pronouns; avoid heterosexist assumptions.
10 Traps of Interviewing: 1) False reassurance, 2) Unwanted advice, 3) Using authority, 4) Avoidance language, 5) Distancing, 6) Professional jargon, 7) Leading questions, 8) Talking too much, 9) Interrupting, 10) "Why" questions (implies blame).

5. Mental Status Exam (MSE) & Assessment

The 4 Components (A-B-C-T):
  • Appearance: Posture, movements, dress, grooming.
  • Behaviour: Level of consciousness, facial expression, speech, mood/affect.
  • Cognition: Orientation, attention span, memory, new learning (Four Unrelated Words).
  • Thought Processes: Perceptions (hallucinations), content (delusions), process (logic), insight/judgement.
  • Key Screenings: Always assess risk for suicide, assault/homicide, and elopement.
  • Cognitive Tools: Mini-Cog, MoCA, RUDAS (screens for dementia/cognitive impairment).

6. Documentation & SBAR Communication

Standardized verbal communication framework:
S Situation: What is happening right now? (Name, patient, room, severe problem).
B Background: Pertinent data related to the moment (admitting diagnosis, vitals).
A Assessment: What do you think is happening? (Severity, involved body system).
R Recommendation: What do you want the physician/team to do? (Order meds, assess).
