Legal Implications & Infection Control

Nursing Exam Study Sheet

PART 1: LEGAL LIMITS AND LIABILITY IN NURSING

  • The 4 Pillars of Nursing: Regulator (protects public), Professional Association (advances profession), Educational (represents programs), Union (supports nurses/working conditions).
  • Fiduciary Relationship: A professional relationship where the nurse provides services causing the patient to trust their specialized knowledge, obligating the nurse to provide safe, competent care.
  • Standards of Care: Legal guidelines establishing expectations for safe care (originating from statutes, regulators, professional orgs, and facility policies).

Torts (Civil Wrongs)

Intentional Torts (Willful acts violating rights):
  • Assault: Conduct creating fear of imminent harmful contact (e.g., threatening an injection).
  • Battery: Intentional physical contact without consent.
  • Invasion of Privacy: Unwanted intrusion, including unauthorized release of medical info.
  • False Imprisonment: Unjustified use of restraints or preventing a patient from leaving.
Unintentional Torts (Negligence/Malpractice): Conduct failing to meet standard of care.
Criteria for Liability (Must prove all 4):
  1. Nurse owed a duty of care.
  2. Nurse breached that duty.
  3. Patient was injured.
  4. Nurse's breach caused the injury.

Consent & End-of-Life Decisions

  • Informed Consent Elements: 1) Capacity, 2) Adequate Information (risks/benefits/alternatives), 3) Voluntariness (no coercion).
  • MAiD Criteria: 18+ and capable, eligible for publicly funded health services, voluntary request with informed consent, suffering from grievous/irremediable medical condition.
  • Advance Directive: Document outlining treatment preferences and authorizing a proxy (substitute decision maker) if capacity is lost.

PART 2: INFECTION PREVENTION AND CONTROL

The Chain of Infection: Infectious agent → Reservoir → Portal of exit → Mode of transmission → Portal of entry → Susceptible host
Modes of Transmission:
Contact: Direct (skin-to-skin) or Indirect (contaminated objects).
Droplet: Large particles propelled up to 2m (cough/sneeze).
Airborne: Small suspended particles inhaled over long distances.
Vehicle/Vectorborne: Contaminated source (water/food) or pests.
Stages of Infection: Incubation (pathogen enters to first symptom) → Prodromal (nonspecific to specific symptoms) → Illness (specific signs manifest) → Convalescence (acute symptoms disappear, recovery).

Body Defenses & HAIs

  • Normal Flora / Body Systems: Good microbes inhibiting pathogens; Skin, cilia, saliva, gastric acid.
  • Inflammation: Vascular/cellular response delivering fluids/blood/nutrients to injured tissue.
  • Health Care–Associated Infections (HAIs): Infections acquired post-admission. Can be exogenous (external) or endogenous (overgrowth of altered normal flora). C. diff is highly common.

Asepsis & Precautions

  • Medical Asepsis (Clean Technique): Reduces/prevents spread of microbes (hand hygiene, clean gloves).
  • Surgical Asepsis (Sterile Technique): Eliminates ALL microorganisms, including spores. Rule: A sterile object remains sterile ONLY when touched by another sterile object.
Decontamination Levels:
  • Cleaning: Physical removal using water/soap.
  • Disinfection: Eliminates all pathogens except bacterial spores.
  • Sterilization: Destroys ALL microorganisms, including spores.
Isolation Precautions:
  • Tier 1 (Routine): Hand hygiene and barrier protection if exposed to blood/fluids/nonintact skin. Used for ALL patients.
  • Tier 2 (Additional):
    • Airborne: Private negative-pressure room, N95 respirator (e.g., TB, measles).
    • Droplet: Private room, mask worn within 2m (e.g., influenza, pertussis).
    • Contact: Private room, gown/gloves upon entry (e.g., MRSA, C. diff).
