Maternity & Developmental Nursing

Core Concepts Exam Study Guide

1. Normal Pregnancy & Adaptation

  • Trimesters: First (weeks 1–13), Second (weeks 14–26), and Third (weeks 27–term).
  • Nagele’s Rule (Estimating Due Date): Subtract 3 months from the first day of the last menstrual period (LMP), add 7 days and 1 year.
  • Maternal Adaptation: Involves accepting the pregnancy, identifying with the mother role, reordering personal relationships, establishing a relationship with the fetus, and preparing for birth.
Paternal Adaptation Phases:
  • Announcement phase: Accepting the biological reality of pregnancy.
  • Moratorium phase: Adjusting to the reality of pregnancy.
  • Focusing phase: Active involvement in the pregnancy and preparing for parenthood.

2. Fetal Assessment & Diagnostic Testing

  • Ultrasound: Gold standard for dating a pregnancy in the first trimester. Assesses fetal growth, anatomy, amniotic fluid volume, and placental position.
  • Amniocentesis: Aspiration of amniotic fluid (after 14 weeks) to diagnose genetic disorders, congenital anomalies, and assess fetal lung maturity.
  • Chorionic Villus Sampling (CVS): Removal of tissue from the placenta (ideally 10–13 weeks) for early genetic diagnosis.
  • Nonstress Test (NST): Assesses fetal well-being. Normal/reassuring = at least 2 fetal heart rate accelerations of 15 bpm lasting 15 seconds within a 20-minute period.
  • Contraction Stress Test (CST): Evaluates fetal response to contractions. Negative (normal) = no late decelerations. Positive (abnormal) = repetitive late decelerations (uteroplacental insufficiency).
  • Biophysical Profile (BPP): Ultrasound + NST evaluating breathing, movements, tone, and amniotic fluid volume. Score < 6 indicates need for immediate intervention/delivery.

3. High-Risk Gestational Conditions

  • Pre-Eclampsia: Hypertension (≥140/90 mm Hg) developing after 20 weeks gestation accompanied by new-onset proteinuria or other end-organ dysfunction.
    • Severe Pre-Eclampsia: BP ≥160/110 mm Hg; requires immediate treatment to prevent seizures.
    • Treatment: Magnesium Sulphate is the drug of choice to prevent/control eclamptic seizures. Monitor for toxicity: loss of reflexes, respiratory depression, oliguria. Antidote is calcium gluconate.
  • HELLP Syndrome: Severe variant of pre-eclampsia characterized by Hemolysis, Elevated Liver enzymes, and Low Platelets.
  • Gestational Diabetes Mellitus (GDM): Hyperglycemia first recognized during pregnancy. Universal screening between 24–28 weeks. Increases fetal risks for macrosomia, hypoglycemia, and IUGR.
  • Hyperemesis Gravidarum (HG): Severe nausea/vomiting resulting in ≥5% prepregnancy weight loss, dehydration, and electrolyte imbalances.
Late Pregnancy Bleeding:
Placenta Previa: Placenta implants low, covering/near cervix.
Key sign: Painless bright red vaginal bleeding.
Vaginal exams strictly contraindicated.
Placental Abruption: Premature separation from uterine wall.
Key signs: Sudden intense localized pain, tenderness, bleeding (may be concealed).

4. Postpartum Complications

Postpartum Hemorrhage (PPH): Leading cause of maternal death. The 4 T's of PPH:
  • Tone (Uterine Atony): Marked relaxation of the uterus; most common cause.
  • Trauma: Lacerations, hematomas, or uterine inversion.
  • Tissue: Retained placental fragments.
  • Thrombin: Coagulopathies (e.g., DIC).
Interventions: Firm fundal massage, eliminate bladder distension, administer uterotonics (Oxytocin, Misoprostol, Carboprost). Postpartum Infections:
  • Endometritis: Uterine lining infection, foul-smelling lochia.
  • Mastitis: Unilateral breast infection with chills, fever, local tenderness.
Perinatal Mood Disorders (PMDs):
  • Postpartum Blues: Mild mood swings/tearfulness resolving within 2 weeks.
  • Perinatal Depression: Severe sadness/guilt lasting beyond 2 weeks.
  • Postpartum Psychosis: Psychiatric emergency. Hallucinations, delusions, extreme impulsivity (high risk of suicide/infanticide).

5. Developmental Theories

  • Cognitive Development (Piaget): Assimilation (making sense of new info) and accommodation (adapting existing thinking). Stages: Sensorimotor, Preoperational, Concrete operations, Formal operations.
  • Psychosocial Development (Erikson): 8 stages with core conflicts (e.g., Trust vs. Mistrust in infancy; Autonomy vs. Shame in toddlers; Identity vs. Role Confusion in adolescence).
  • Psychosexual Development (Freud): Regulated by id (impulses), ego (reality), and superego (conscience). Stages: Oral, Anal, Phallic, Latency, Genital.
  • Moral Development (Kohlberg & Gilligan): Kohlberg focuses on justice/absolute rules. Gilligan argues women's moral development focuses heavily on interpersonal relationships and dependency.
  • Bioecological Theory (Bronfenbrenner): Development depends on layers of environmental interaction: Microsystem (immediate setting), Mesosystem (relationships between settings), Exosystem (social structures), and Macrosystem (cultural values).
